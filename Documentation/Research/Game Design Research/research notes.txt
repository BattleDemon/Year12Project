### Summary

This article by Markus Eldegard Mindrebø explores the innovative use of **relationship-based grand strategy video games**, specifically *Crusader Kings II*, as **simulation tools for qualitative historical network research**. Focusing on medieval aristocratic networks derived from the Old Norse-Icelandic kings’ sagas (12th–13th centuries), the study demonstrates how such games can model and analyze the formation, development, and dissolution of interpersonal networks in a **dynamic, interactive, and temporal framework**.

---

### Key Insights and Concepts

- **Historical Network Analysis (HNA)** traditionally struggles with static snapshots of networks; this study proposes **using complex strategy games with AI-driven NPCs as dynamic simulators** to explore network progression and agent motivations over time.
- *Crusader Kings II* (CKII) was chosen due to its:
  - Detailed **character traits system** influencing socio-political behavior.
  - Complex **opinion mechanics** quantifying interpersonal relationships numerically.
  - Realistic modeling of **medieval kinship, alliances, conflicts, and succession** within a feudal context.
- NPCs in CKII act as **autonomous agents**, fulfilling criteria for agent-based modeling (ABM), allowing researchers to examine **individual decision-making within broader social networks**.
- The article emphasizes a **hybrid methodology** combining game simulation with traditional historical methods to verify findings and generate new research questions.

### Theoretical Framework and Methodology

| Aspect                                | Description                                                                                   |
|-------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Agent-Based Modeling (ABM)**      | Simulation of autonomous agents interacting within an environment, useful for socio-political hierarchies analysis. |
| **Social Network Analysis (SNA)**   | Mapping and studying connections between individuals, traditionally static but extended here through simulation.   |
| **Game as Network Software**        | CKII serves as a platform to reconstruct medieval networks, allowing manipulation of variables and repeated modeling. |
| **Reduction and Assumptions**       | Focus on Scandinavian kingdoms; interpersonal connections prioritized; agents pursue pragmatic Realpolitik with emotional modifiers. |
| **Simulation Approaches**            | Passive observation, player intervention (partial or full), and developer console usage to guide or verify network dynamics. |
| **Verification Phase**               | Comparing simulation outcomes to saga narratives to identify alternative actions or motivations behind historical events. |

---

### Case Study: Simulation of Norwegian Aristocratic Networks (c. 936)

- The study uses *Hákonar saga góða* from *Heimskringla* (Snorri Sturluson’s kings’ sagas) as the core historiographical source.
- The historical context involves:
  - Haraldr Fairhair’s dynasty aftermath.
  - Struggle for Norwegian kingship between brothers Eiríkr Blood-axe and Hákon the Good.
  - External influences from Denmark, Orkney, and England.
- CKII’s 936 start date used to recreate political landscape, kinship ties, and power structures.

---

### Simulation Runs and Findings

| Simulation Part          | Method                                                  | Key Findings                                                                                                                  |
|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **1. Observing Succession** | Passive observation via console mode                    | Hákon consolidates power with strong aristocratic support; Eiríkr remains in exile without challenging Norway directly; Danish support fluctuates, impacting Eiríkr’s chances. |
| **2. Preventing Succession** | Player controls Hákon, aims for stability               | Successfully maintains network cohesion and prevents Eiríkr’s challenge; confirms importance of internal alliances and religious appeasement. |
| **3. Supporting Succession** | Player controls Danish king Haraldr Bluetooth to back Eiríkr | Alliance formed but Eiríkr still does not press claim on Norway, reinforcing idea that Gunnhildr may be key strategist behind sons’ ambitions. |
| **4. Forcing Succession** | Artificially removes Hákon and Eiríkr to place Haraldr Eiríksson as king | Initial peaceful succession followed by tyrannical moves by Haraldr and Gunnhildr provoke aristocratic revolt, culminating in rebellion led by Hákon jarl Sigurðarson, aligning with saga narratives. |

### Advantages and Limitations of Using CKII for Historical Network Simulation

**Advantages:**

- Enables analysis of **emergent social structures** beyond fixed historiographical narratives.
- Allows exploration of **“what-if” scenarios** by modifying agent attributes or network ties.
- Rich **trait and opinion system** closely mirrors medieval interpersonal motivations described in sagas.
- Facilitates **dynamic temporal analysis** of network evolution.

**Limitations:**

- Game designed primarily for entertainment; **historical accuracy is imperfect**.
- Simplifications exist in succession laws and social hierarchy, especially in Norwegian context.
- Peripheral actors and less powerful network members often **underrepresented**, limiting micro-level analyses.
- Requires **modification and scholarly input** for improved historical fidelity.

---

### Conclusions and Future Directions

- The study establishes a **proof-of-concept for using grand strategy games as heuristic tools** in historical network research.
- **Deviations from historical narratives in simulations are especially valuable**, revealing alternative courses of action and underlying motivations.
- The approach offers a method to **“fill in” fragmented or incomplete historical data**, addressing “broken links” in medieval networks.
- Future work should focus on:
  - Developing **customized game mods** to fit specific historiographical contexts.
  - Incorporating **more actors and social strata** for comprehensive analysis.
  - Extending methodology to other medieval sources and regions.
- Ultimately, this approach bridges **digital gaming, social network theory, and medieval historiography**, enriching each field’s understanding of past social and political complexities.
